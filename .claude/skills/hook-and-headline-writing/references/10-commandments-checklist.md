# 10 Commandments of Engagement Checklist

A systematic checklist for optimizing hooks and headlines based on proven psychological principles.

## How to Use This Checklist

**Purpose:** Evaluate and improve your top 5-7 hook variations before final selection

**Workflow:**
1. Generate 10+ hook variations (Phase 2 of main skill)
2. Select top 5-7 based on frameworks used
3. Run each through this checklist
4. Score: Count how many commandments each hook applies (aim for 4-6)
5. Optimize: Add missing commandments where natural
6. Select: Choose hook with highest score + best fit

**Important:** You don't need all 10 in every hook. Aim for 4-6. More than 7 risks feeling manipulative.

## Commandment 1: Numbers and Statistics

### Principle
Use specific numbers to add credibility and reduce vagueness

### Why It Works
- Brain processes numbers faster than words
- Specificity implies accuracy and research
- Concrete vs abstract (easier to visualize)
- Creates curiosity ("Why that specific number?")

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does the hook include a specific number?
- [ ] Are percentages, dollar amounts, or timeframes included?
- [ ] Have I avoided vague words like "many," "some," "lots"?
- [ ] Is the number realistic and accurate?

### Examples

**‚ùå Without numbers:**
- "Many businesses are using this strategy now"
- "Lots of people are making this mistake"
- "Save time with these tips"

**‚úÖ With numbers:**
- "1.5 million businesses are using this strategy now"
- "90% of new founders make this mistake"
- "Save 100+ hours with these 7 tips"

### Types of Numbers to Use
- Percentages: "76% satisfaction rate"
- Dollar amounts: "$12,000 saved per year"
- Timeframes: "In 90 days"
- Quantities: "7 strategies"
- Specific results: "From 100 to 10,000 followers"
- Ratios: "1 person, 8 clients"

### When to Skip
- Number would be misleading or inaccurate
- Context is purely qualitative
- Too many numbers create clutter

## Commandment 2: Negativity Bias

### Principle
Highlight potential losses, mistakes, or negative consequences

### Why It Works
- Humans are wired to avoid loss more than seek gain
- Fear of missing out (FOMO) is powerful motivator
- Mistakes are more memorable than successes
- Creates urgency ("I need to fix this")

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does the hook warn of a potential loss?
- [ ] Are consequences of inaction clear?
- [ ] Does it identify what reader might be missing?
- [ ] Is the fear legitimate (not manipulative)?

### Examples

**‚ùå Positive framing only:**
- "How to succeed at freelancing"
- "Great tips for productivity"
- "Build a better business"

**‚úÖ Negativity bias applied:**
- "The costly mistake 90% of freelancers make"
- "What you're missing about deep work"
- "Stop doing this before it kills your business"

### Ways to Apply
- **Mistake focus:** "Are you making this [X] mistake?"
- **Loss aversion:** "What [inaction] is costing you"
- **Warning:** "Before you [action], read this"
- **Missing out:** "What [group] doesn't want you to know"
- **Consequence:** "Why [common practice] is killing [goal]"

### When to Skip
- Content is purely inspirational
- Audience is already anxious (don't add more fear)
- Loss framing would be manipulative

### Balance with Positivity
Don't make everything negative. Best hooks often pair:
- Negative hook + positive solution
- "Stop [problem]. Start [solution]."

## Commandment 3: Pattern Interrupt

### Principle
Challenge common beliefs, practices, or expectations

### Why It Works
- Breaks through scrolling/scanning autopilot
- Creates cognitive dissonance ("Wait, what?")
- Signals non-obvious content
- Makes reader pause and reconsider

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does the hook challenge a common assumption?
- [ ] Would it make someone stop mid-scroll?
- [ ] Is it surprising or counterintuitive?
- [ ] Does it contradict conventional wisdom?

### Examples

**‚ùå Expected/conventional:**
- "Why you should plan your content calendar"
- "How to grow your audience effectively"
- "The importance of credentials"

**‚úÖ Pattern interrupt:**
- "Forget everything you know about content strategy"
- "The less you plan, the more you ship"
- "Why the best founders have zero traditional credentials"

### Ways to Apply
- **Forget everything:** "Forget what you know about [topic]"
- **Opposite claim:** "The less [X], the more [Y]"
- **Surprising stat:** "98% don't know [surprising fact]"
- **Challenge authority:** "[Experts] are wrong about [topic]"
- **Reverse conventional wisdom:** "Why [common practice] actually hurts"

### Types of Pattern Interrupts

**1. Reversal:**
"The less you post, the more you grow"

**2. Negation:**
"Stop tracking vanity metrics. Start tracking revenue."

**3. Paradox:**
"The hardest path is often the easiest"

**4. Contradiction:**
"My business grew 10x when I started working 4 hours a day"

### When to Skip
- Content doesn't actually challenge assumptions
- You're being contrarian just for attention
- The interrupt would confuse rather than intrigue

## Commandment 4: Target Callout

### Principle
Directly address your specific audience by name or situation

### Why It Works
- Immediate self-selection ("This is for me")
- Increases relevance perception
- Cuts through noise for niche audiences
- Shows you understand them specifically

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does the hook name the target audience?
- [ ] Would readers immediately know if it's for them?
- [ ] Is the audience specific (not just "entrepreneurs" or "people")?
- [ ] Does it reference their unique context?

### Examples

**‚ùå Generic audience:**
- "Tips for better productivity"
- "How to grow a business"
- "Advice for professionals"

**‚úÖ Target callout:**
- "Solopreneurs: Stop trading time for money"
- "Burned-out corporate employees: Here's your exit plan"
- "For creators considering going full-time:"

### Ways to Apply

**1. Direct address:**
"[Audience name]: [Message]"
- "Remote workers: The productivity system that actually works"

**2. Situation callout:**
"If you're [specific situation], [message]"
- "If your business is stuck at $10K/month, read this"

**3. Pain point specificity:**
"For [audience] struggling with [specific problem]"
- "For freelancers drowning in client work"

**4. Identity-based:**
"[Role/identity] need to know this:"
- "Senior developers who secretly hate managing people"

### Levels of Specificity

**Too broad:**
- "Entrepreneurs" (which entrepreneurs?)
- "Marketers" (what kind? what context?)

**Good:**
- "Solo consultants"
- "First-time founders"

**Very specific (often best):**
- "Solo consultants in their first 90 days"
- "Burned-out agency owners considering going solo"

### When to Skip
- Content applies to everyone equally
- Being too specific would exclude valuable readers
- Platform/context makes audience obvious

## Commandment 5: Problem Callout

### Principle
Identify the specific pain point, struggle, or obstacle immediately

### Why It Works
- Shows you understand their struggle
- Creates instant relatability
- Signals relevant solution incoming
- Pain is more motivating than gain initially

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does the hook name a specific problem?
- [ ] Would readers recognize this pain point?
- [ ] Is it concrete (not vague like "doing better")?
- [ ] Does it tap into real frustration?

### Examples

**‚ùå Vague or no problem:**
- "Tips for business growth"
- "How to improve marketing"
- "Better productivity approaches"

**‚úÖ Problem callout:**
- "Tired of posting daily with zero engagement?"
- "Drowning in tools you never use?"
- "Spending $15K/month on ads with no ROI?"

### Ways to Apply

**1. Question format:**
"Tired of [specific problem]?"
- "Tired of endless meetings that accomplish nothing?"

**2. Pain statement:**
"[Problem] is killing [goal]"
- "Perfectionism is killing your productivity"

**3. Struggle acknowledgment:**
"Struggling with [specific issue]?"
- "Struggling to find clients who pay what you're worth?"

**4. Frustration naming:**
"The frustrating truth about [problem]"
- "The frustrating truth about 'passive income'"

### Problem Specificity Levels

**Too vague:**
- "Having trouble?"
- "Need help?"

**Good:**
- "Struggling with sales?"
- "Content not converting?"

**Very specific (often best):**
- "Spending 3 hours on emails that get no replies?"
- "Your 'viral' post got 10 likes from other creators?"

### Problem + Solution Pattern
Best hooks name problem, then promise solution:
- "Drowning in tasks? Here's the simple filter I use:"
- "Clients ghosting you? This changed everything:"

### When to Skip
- Content is aspirational/inspirational (not problem-focused)
- Problem would be offensive or insensitive
- Audience doesn't identify with having a "problem"

## Commandment 6: Confidence and Conviction

### Principle
Use strong, assertive language; eliminate hedge words

### Why It Works
- Confidence is persuasive
- Certainty reduces decision friction
- Hedge words signal doubt
- Reader wants decisive guidance, not maybes

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Have I eliminated hedge words (maybe, might, could, possibly)?
- [ ] Do I speak in absolutes when accurate?
- [ ] Is the language assertive, not tentative?
- [ ] Would I say this with confidence to a friend?

### Examples

**‚ùå Hedging/uncertain:**
- "This might help you grow faster"
- "Possibly the best approach"
- "Could be useful for scaling"
- "I think maybe this works"

**‚úÖ Confident/convicted:**
- "This is how to grow faster"
- "The best approach for scaling"
- "Use this for breakthrough results"
- "This works. Here's why:"

### Hedge Words to Eliminate

**Remove these:**
- Maybe, might, could, possibly, probably, perhaps
- I think, I believe, in my opinion (when unnecessary)
- Sort of, kind of, somewhat, fairly
- Try to, attempt to (just say "do")

**Replace with:**
- Is, will, does, works
- This is how, here's why, the truth is
- Simply, just, exactly

### Conviction Techniques

**1. Speak in absolutes (when accurate):**
- Not: "This could increase revenue"
- But: "This increases revenue"

**2. Use declarative statements:**
- Not: "I think hustle culture might not work"
- But: "Hustle culture doesn't work. Here's what does:"

**3. Command/imperative voice:**
- Not: "You might want to try..."
- But: "Do this:"

**4. Remove qualifiers:**
- Not: "This is a relatively simple approach"
- But: "This is simple"

### When to Add Nuance
- Legal/medical topics requiring disclaimers
- Complex topics needing context
- When absolute claim would be dishonest

### Confidence ‚â† Arrogance
**Confident:** "This is the system that works"
**Arrogant:** "This is the only system that works, everyone else is wrong"

## Commandment 7: Aesthetics

### Principle
Ensure visual appeal and readability

### Why It Works
- Visual processing happens before reading
- Clean formatting signals quality
- Scannable structure invites reading
- Wall of text triggers avoidance

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Is the hook visually clean?
- [ ] Are line breaks in the right places?
- [ ] Does it look easy to read at a glance?
- [ ] Would I stop scrolling based on how this looks?

### Examples

**‚ùå Poor aesthetics:**
```
I've spent 10 years building businesses and I've learned so much about what works and what doesn't work and I want to share the top 7 strategies that actually made a difference in our growth journey.
```
(Wall of text, no breaks, one sentence)

**‚úÖ Good aesthetics:**
```
10 years building businesses.

Here are the 7 strategies that actually worked:
```
(Short lines, clear breaks, scannable)

### Formatting Rules

**For Social Media Hooks (X, LinkedIn, Facebook):**

1. **First line must be short** (5-12 words ideal)
2. **Use line breaks between sentences**
3. **Max 3 sentences for hook**
4. **Avoid ALL CAPS** (except strategic emphasis)
5. **Use emojis sparingly** (1-2 max, if at all)

**For Newsletter Subject Lines:**

1. **35-50 characters ideal** (mobile preview)
2. **No line breaks** (won't work in subject lines)
3. **Clear even when truncated**

**For Headlines:**

1. **One line when possible**
2. **Use title case or sentence case consistently**
3. **Avoid punctuation clusters** (!!!, ???)

### Visual Hierarchy

**Good pattern:**
```
[Short hook line]

[Context sentence]

[Promise/benefit]
```

**Bad pattern:**
```
[Everything crammed together without breaks and no visual hierarchy making it hard to scan or know where to focus attention]
```

### Whitespace Matters
More whitespace = easier to read = higher engagement

**Compare:**

Cramped:
```
10 years consulting. 5 startups. 150 clients. Here's what worked:
```

Spacious:
```
10 years consulting.
5 startups.
150 clients.

Here's what worked:
```

The spacious version performs better on social media.

### Platform-Specific Aesthetics

**X/Twitter:**
- Very short first line (hook)
- Generous line breaks
- Emoji at end of thread signal (üßµ)

**LinkedIn:**
- First 2 lines show in feed (must work standalone)
- Professional formatting
- Line breaks for readability

**Facebook:**
- Can be denser (platform shows more)
- Questions work well
- Image + text combo ideal

### When to Skip
Subject lines and contexts where formatting doesn't display (plain text email, etc.)

## Commandment 8: Potential Benefit

### Principle
Clearly state what the reader will gain

### Why It Works
- Answers "What's in it for me?" immediately
- Reduces uncertainty about value
- Benefit > Feature (people care about outcomes)
- Clear ROI compels click

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Is the benefit crystal clear?
- [ ] Does it focus on outcome, not just process?
- [ ] Can reader visualize their life after?
- [ ] Is benefit specific and tangible?

### Examples

**‚ùå Unclear/no benefit:**
- "Things about productivity"
- "Business information"
- "Growth strategies"

**‚úÖ Clear benefit:**
- "How to cut your workweek by 20 hours"
- "The system that ended our sales slump forever"
- "7 tools that gave me 10 hours back per week"

### Benefit Types

**1. Time saved:**
- "Save 100 hours of research"
- "Cut planning time in half"

**2. Money saved/earned:**
- "Save $12,000/year"
- "How I earn $30K/month with 8 clients"

**3. Problem solved:**
- "End the feast-or-famine cycle"
- "Finally get consistent leads"

**4. Outcome achieved:**
- "Clients who actually pay on time"
- "From hating sales to closing deals confidently"

**5. Stress reduced:**
- "Stop feeling overwhelmed"
- "The guilt-free approach to growth"

**6. Transformation:**
- "From burned out to thriving"
- "Struggling creator to full-time income"

### Feature ‚Üí Benefit Translation

**Feature:** "Automated email sequences"
**Benefit:** "Wake up to new sales without lifting a finger"

**Feature:** "Small group coaching"
**Benefit:** "Get help the moment you're stuck"

**Feature:** "Flexible schedule"
**Benefit:** "Work when you want, not when you have to"

### Specificity Levels

**Vague benefit:**
- "Better results"
- "Improved performance"
- "More success"

**Specific benefit:**
- "2x your close rate in 6 months"
- "From constant hustle to 4-hour workdays"
- "6-figure income with no employees"

### When to Skip
- Benefit is so obvious it's redundant
- Content is entertaining (not utility-focused)
- Audience reads for identity/philosophy (not outcomes)

## Commandment 9: Social Proof

### Principle
Leverage authority, popularity, or third-party validation

### Why It Works
- Reduces risk perception
- Borrowed credibility
- "If it worked for them..." (vicarious success)
- Authority shortcut to trust

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does the hook reference credible authority?
- [ ] Are results/credentials mentioned?
- [ ] Is third-party validation included?
- [ ] Would this build trust with skeptical reader?

### Examples

**‚ùå No social proof:**
- "How to grow a business"
- "Tips for freelancing"
- "Marketing strategies that work"

**‚úÖ With social proof:**
- "How Alex Hormozi grew Gym Launch to $100M"
- "The system 10,000+ freelancers use"
- "Backed by 20 years of research"

### Types of Social Proof

**1. Authority credentials:**
- "15 years in the industry"
- "Former VP at Google"
- "Built and sold 3 companies"

**2. Results achieved:**
- "Grew from 5 to 150 clients"
- "Saved $12,000 per year"
- "6 exits, none VC-backed"

**3. Scale/popularity:**
- "Used by 10,000+ businesses"
- "Trusted by 500+ agencies"
- "1.5 million downloads"

**4. Third-party validation:**
- "Featured in The New York Times"
- "Backed by Harvard research"
- "Recommended by industry leaders"

**5. Expert endorsement:**
- "Naval Ravikant's approach to wealth"
- "What Tim Ferriss says about productivity"
- "Seth Godin's method"

**6. User results:**
- "How @username went from X to Y"
- "The system that worked for [name]"

### When to Include

‚úÖ **Include when:**
- You have relevant credentials/results
- Borrowing authority from respected figure
- Scale adds legitimacy
- Results are impressive and relevant

‚ùå **Skip when:**
- Would feel like bragging
- Credentials aren't relevant
- No meaningful social proof to share
- Audience doesn't care about authority

### Bragging vs Authority

**Authority (good):**
"10 years building businesses. Here's what worked:"

**Bragging (bad):**
"I'm the best entrepreneur ever. Let me tell you why:"

### Combining Social Proof Types

"How Sara Blakely (founder of Spanx) grew from selling fax machines to building a billion-dollar brand"

This combines:
- Authority (founder)
- Results (billion-dollar)
- Transformation (fax machines ‚Üí empire)

## Commandment 10: Warning and Caution

### Principle
Create sense of urgency or importance through warning

### Why It Works
- Signals critical information
- Creates FOMO (fear of missing out)
- Implies reader might be at risk
- Urgency compels immediate action

### Checklist Questions
- [ ] Does it suggest reader might be missing something?
- [ ] Is there time sensitivity or urgency?
- [ ] Does it warn of potential pitfall?
- [ ] Would it make someone prioritize reading this?

### Examples

**‚ùå No urgency:**
- "Tips for marketing"
- "How businesses grow"
- "Information about sales"

**‚úÖ Warning/urgency:**
- "Before you hire an agency, read this"
- "The mistake that costs freelancers $100K+"
- "Warning: This common practice is killing your conversions"

### Ways to Apply

**1. "Before you..." format:**
- "Before you launch, ask these 3 questions"
- "Before you quit your job, read this"

**2. "Warning:" prefix:**
- "Warning: This common pricing mistake costs you $1000s"
- "Warning: Not all automation is created equal"

**3. Time urgency:**
- "Applications close November 10"
- "This is changing fast"
- "Don't wait until [consequence]"

**4. Consequence framing:**
- "What [inaction] is costing you"
- "The hidden cost of [common practice]"

**5. Mistake prevention:**
- "The costly mistake 90% make"
- "Avoid these 3 pitfalls"
- "Don't make the mistake I did"

### Types of Urgency

**1. Time-based:**
"Applications close November 10"

**2. Consequence-based:**
"Every day without this system costs you leads"

**3. Opportunity-based:**
"Spots are filling fast"

**4. Information-based:**
"What you don't know about [topic] is hurting you"

### When to Use

‚úÖ **Use when:**
- There IS genuine urgency
- Consequence of delay is real
- Want to move reader from awareness to action
- Information is time-sensitive

‚ùå **Don't use when:**
- Creating false urgency
- Content is evergreen/not time-sensitive
- Would feel manipulative
- Audience is already anxious (don't add more pressure)

### Ethical Guidelines

**Real urgency:** "Applications close November 10" ‚úÖ
**False urgency:** "Read this NOW or fail forever" ‚ùå

**Helpful warning:** "Before you buy, here's what to know" ‚úÖ
**Manipulative:** "If you don't do this, you're a failure" ‚ùå

## Scoring Your Hooks

### How to Score

For each hook variation, count how many commandments apply:

**10/10:** Probably overkill, may feel manipulative
**7-9/10:** Very strong, high engagement likely
**4-6/10:** Good, aim for this range
**2-3/10:** Weak, needs optimization
**0-1/10:** Generic, start over

### Example Scoring

**Hook:** "1.5 million businesses are using this now. Here's the surprising truth about why it works:"

Commandments applied:
1. ‚úÖ Numbers (1.5 million)
2. ‚ùå Negativity bias
3. ‚úÖ Pattern interrupt ("surprising truth")
4. ‚ùå Target callout
5. ‚ùå Problem callout
6. ‚úÖ Confidence ("Here's")
7. ‚úÖ Aesthetics (clean, short)
8. ‚úÖ Benefit (will learn "why it works")
9. ‚úÖ Social proof (scale = 1.5M)
10. ‚ùå Warning

**Score: 6/10** - Strong hook

### Optimization Strategy

If score is 3 or below, ask:
1. Can I add a specific number?
2. Can I name the target audience?
3. Can I make the benefit clearer?
4. Can I add urgency/warning?

Don't force all 10. Natural application > checklist completion.

## Platform-Specific Applications

### X/Twitter Hooks
**Prioritize:** Numbers, Pattern Interrupt, Confidence, Aesthetics
**Often skip:** Target callout (if audience is already niche)

### LinkedIn Hooks
**Prioritize:** Social Proof, Confidence, Benefit, Target Callout
**Often skip:** Warning/urgency (unless B2B relevant)

### Newsletter Subject Lines
**Prioritize:** Numbers, Benefit, Curiosity (pattern interrupt), Warning
**Often skip:** Aesthetics (no formatting), Social Proof (less space)

### Facebook Hooks
**Prioritize:** Problem, Target, Benefit, Aesthetics
**Often skip:** Formal social proof (casual platform)
